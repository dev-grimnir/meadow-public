Management practices

  • No insecticides or pesticides. Host plants only work if caterpillars survive on them.
  • Stems stand year-round, on purpose. Swallowtails overwinter as chrysalises on standing stems and emerge roughly April–June; the stems also shelter beneficial insects and hold winter seed for native birds. This is recognized conservation practice, not neglect.
  • Dense patch planting, mapped bands. Species are placed by mature height, juglone tolerance, and butterfly value — see the planting map and the design rationale.
  • Fall direct-sow method. Deep-taproot natives are established by post-frost surface sowing with winter stratification, per the written planting calendar.
  • Straight species, northeast ecotype. No cultivars; seed sourced for local adaptation.

Ordinance compliance — IPMC §302.4

North Huntingdon Township enforces IPMC §302.4 (Weeds), which restricts plant growth over 12 inches but exempts cultivated flowers and gardens. This planting is an intentionally cultivated garden within that exemption. The evidence of deliberate cultivation and management:

  • A mapped planting layout — defined bands by height, juglone tolerance, and butterfly value (interactive map).
  • A defined plant palette — every species identified and documented (species table).
  • A written planting & maintenance calendar — clearing, sowing, and seasonal tending (planting plan).
  • A public, dated record — every change to the garden and its plan is a timestamped commit in the repository.

The full argument, including the standing-stem rationale, is in compliance.md.
